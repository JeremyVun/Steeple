# Hallspace
### Finding a home for community — matching churches with the people who need their space

*A plain-language overview of what Hallspace is, who it's for, and where it's going. (A separate, more technical specification exists for the people building it.)*

---

Hallspace is a simple idea with a big heart. Thousands of churches have beautiful halls, rooms, and kitchens that sit empty most of the week — while right around the corner, playgroups, tutoring programs, hobby clubs, and small non-profits struggle to find an affordable place to meet. **Hallspace connects the two:** a friendly, local, community-first way to find space — and to offer it — starting at free.

## Where this came from

In 2017, while scouting a location for a school's next campus, Hallspace's founder found herself driving door to door, knocking on churches, trying to work out by hand whether any of their buildings might suit. It was slow, awkward work — and along the way she discovered something surprising: **the churches *wanted* to be found.** Several asked her to "spread the word" about space they had going spare. Both sides were quietly looking for each other, and there was simply no way for them to meet.

Hallspace is the bridge that didn't exist that day.

## The problem — felt on both sides

**Churches have space, and increasingly need it to work harder for them.**
- The average church building is in use only about **15% of the week** — effectively empty six days out of seven.¹
- Those buildings are expensive to keep: roughly **$7–$10 per square foot every year**, just to operate.²
- And the pressure is real and growing. In 2024, **more U.S. churches closed than opened** — about 4,000 closures against 3,800 openings — and many congregations are actively looking for respectful ways to earn a little from their space.³

A hall that hosts a weekday playgroup isn't just extra income for a church — it's the church living out its mission, right in its own neighbourhood.

**Community organizers have the opposite problem: they need space, and can't easily find it.**
- Northern Virginia is full of potential users — **roughly 47,000 registered non-profits across Virginia**, and **around 480 public schools** across the five NoVA jurisdictions, plus countless playgroups, clubs, classes, and faith groups.⁴
- Affordable meeting and activity space is a well-documented scarcity — so much so that community foundations around the country run dedicated programs *just* to help non-profits find free or low-cost rooms.⁵
- Today, finding that space still means doing exactly what the founder did in 2017: asking around, cold-calling, and hoping. The only polished alternatives are commercial event-venue websites priced for weddings and corporate offsites — not a Tuesday-morning toddler group.

## What Hallspace does

Hallspace is a **community-first marketplace for local space.** Churches list the rooms and halls they're willing to share — for free, or for a modest community rate. Organizers search what's nearby, see real photos and details, and send a friendly request. The church sees who's asking and what for, and says yes (or asks a question, or says no). The door-knocking, replaced by a map.

Two things make it unlike anything else out there:

1. **It's community-first, not commercial.** Most space-rental platforms exist to turn a church's building into a revenue machine — pitching "passive income" and chasing commercial renters. Hallspace leads with mission and belonging instead, and **prices start at $0.** Free space isn't a loss here — it's the whole point. It's what a church most wants to offer, and what a stretched playgroup most needs.
2. **It's radically local.** Unlike booking a holiday rental, both the organizer *and* the families who'll attend are tied to a place. Hallspace is built around that — it's about finding the *right* space within reach, not the *best* space anywhere.

## Who it's for

**The church (the host).** A congregation with a hall that's quiet on weekdays and would love to see it full of life — but wants to know who's coming and what they'll be doing. Hallspace gives them that control: every request shows the group, the activity, the size, and how often, and nothing happens without their yes.

**The organizer (the guest).** Picture **Maria**, who runs a weekly toddler playgroup. She needs a hall every Tuesday morning, close to home, with a bathroom and some open floor — ideally free, or close to it. Today she'd be making phone calls. With Hallspace she opens the map, filters to what she needs, finds St. Andrew's hall a few minutes away, sends a request, and is set up for the term. Twelve toddlers show up on Tuesday; it just works — and next week it repeats.

## The experiences we'll build

To meet people where they are, Hallspace is more than a single app:

- **A website anyone can browse — no download required.** This is the front door. Asking someone to install an app just to *look* is a real barrier, so you'll be able to see a hall, share it with a friend by link, and get drawn in — and only reach for the app when you're ready to book.
- **A mobile app (iPhone and Android).** The home for engaged organizers and churches — browse the map, send and manage requests, stay in the loop.
- **Instant notifications.** So a church knows the moment a request arrives, and an organizer knows the moment they're approved.
- **Email updates.** A reliable backstop for the moments that matter — a new request, an approval — so nothing slips through the cracks.
- **A simple behind-the-scenes tool** for the founder to add churches and keep everything running smoothly in the early days.

## How it works, start to finish

1. Maria hears about Hallspace and **browses nearby halls on the website** — no sign-up, no download.
2. She filters to what she needs: *Tuesday mornings, ~15 people, toddler-friendly, free or low-cost.* A few church halls appear within range.
3. She opens **St. Andrew's** — photos, capacity, what's included, the house rules.
4. She **sends a request**, describing her group and that it's a weekly booking, and signs in (one tap, with Google or Apple).
5. The **church is notified**, sees who's asking and why, and **approves**.
6. The booking is confirmed for the term; both sides are notified. Maria has her space.
7. The playgroup runs, week after week — and Maria can leave a rating that builds her good standing for next time.

## The market — and why this lane is wide open

There's one company worth knowing about: **ChurchSpace**, often called "the Airbnb for churches." It's real and funded — it's raised around **$1.48 million** — and some of its churches earn **$23,000–$100,000 a year**.⁶ But here's the crucial difference: ChurchSpace sells churches *passive income.* It's pitched around food entrepreneurs, small businesses, and "turning churches into economic engines" — a commercial play, currently focused on Texas and Detroit, not Northern Virginia.

That leaves the lane Hallspace cares about — **community-first, free-first, hyper-local** — wide open. Put simply: **everyone else is helping churches make *money* from their space; Hallspace helps churches make *impact* with it.** Nobody is doing that — and it happens to be exactly what most churches actually want their buildings to be for.

(The wider "space rental" world — companies like Peerspace and Tagvenue, with tens of thousands of venues between them — is large and growing,⁷ but it's built for commercial events at commercial prices. It doesn't serve, and isn't trying to serve, the Tuesday-morning playgroup.)

## The numbers — kept honest

Hallspace is deliberately **lean and mission-driven — not a venture-capital rocket ship.** That shapes everything, including the money.

**It costs almost nothing to run.** In the early days the whole service can operate for roughly **$20–$30 a month** — a small server, photo storage, a domain, and the Apple developer membership — comfortably under the ~$100/month ceiling we've set ourselves.⁸ That low cost isn't a limitation; it's a superpower. It means Hallspace can give space away for free and still survive, in a way a company burning investor money never could.

**The opportunity is real, even locally.** Fairfax County alone has **over 550 churches**;⁹ across the wider NoVA region, well over a thousand. On the other side sit tens of thousands of non-profits and hundreds of schools needing space. Community halls commonly rent in the range of **$25–$75 an hour** for community groups (more for events),¹⁰ so once paid bookings begin to flow, there's a gentle, growing stream of value moving through the platform.

**How it sustains itself — later, not now.** In the early days Hallspace earns nothing, and that's by design: the first goal is simply to prove people love it. Over time, revenue can come quietly and fairly — a small, invisible fee folded into *paid* bookings (free ones stay free), optional "verified" trust features, and, further out, helping churches with practical things like insurance. Money moving through Hallspace will be **one** sign of success — alongside the real one: community activities actually happening that wouldn't have otherwise.

## What success looks like

- **In a few months:** a real, living map of space in one Northern Virginia neighbourhood, with the first churches listed and the first playgroups, classes, and clubs actually meeting.
- **In a year:** a thriving local network where word is spreading on its own, dozens of recurring community activities are running, and the platform comfortably covers its own tiny costs.
- **Longer term:** the same magic, neighbourhood by neighbourhood — knowing clearly that each new area has to be *earned* with real local relationships, the same way the founder will earn the first one.

## The honest part — what we still have to crack

Every good plan has one real risk, and ours is worth saying plainly: **getting the word out to organizers.**

Signing up churches is the part the founder already knows how to do — she's been doing versions of it since 2017. The harder question is how Maria *hears about Hallspace in the first place.* The likeliest answer is the founder's own network — the schools and non-profits she already knows — together with the churches themselves telling their congregations. But this is the piece to figure out together, because the best map in the world is useless if nobody knows to look at it.

Everything else — the trust and safety, the technology, the costs, the design — we have solid answers for. **This one we solve together.**
